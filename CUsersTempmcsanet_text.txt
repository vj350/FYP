MCSANet: A multiscale convolutional self attention network with data
augmentation for motor imagery EEG classiﬁcation
Seema Devi 1 · Kamal Singh 1 · Nitin Singh Singha 1 · Mahesh K. Singh 1
Received: 8 August 2025 / Revised: 16 December 2025 / Accepted: 22 February 2026

Abstract
Attention-based neural networks have advanced EEG decoding for motor imagery (MI) in Brain–Computer Interfaces (BCIs)
by capturing global temporal dependencies, whereas convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are particularly effective in
capturing localized spatio–temporal patterns. Combining both enables an effective extraction of local and global features.
However, ﬁxed kernel sizes in traditional CNNs limit feature diversity and accuracy. To address this, we propose the Multi-
Scale Convolutional Self-Attention Network (MCSANet), which integrates multi-scale CNNs with attention mechanisms and
introduces the Temporal Segment Shufﬂing (TSS) data augmentation strategy to enhance model performance further. Trained
on BCIC-IV -2a and BCIC-IV -2b datasets in both subject-dependent and subject-independent setups, MCSANet achieves
78.11% accuracy (κ = 0.72) on BCIC-IV -2a and 85.56% accuracy (κ = 0.72) on BCIC-IV -2b, outperforming state-of-the-
art methods. TSS further boosts accuracy by 5 .89% and 2.8% on the respective datasets. These results highlight the potential
of MCSANet and TSS for more accurate and robust real-world BCI applications.
Keywords Attention · Brain computer interface · Deep learning · EEG · Motor imagery · Multiscale CNN
1 Introduction
Brain–Computer Interfaces (BCIs) enable direct communi-
cation between the brain and external devices by acquiring,
processing, and translating neural signals into control com-
mands. In the Motor Imagery (MI) paradigm, users mentally
simulate limb movements without physical execution. Elec-
troencephalography (EEG) is widely used for MI-BCIs due
to its portability, affordability, and ease of use [ 1]. These
systems are vital for neural rehabilitation and assistive tech-
nologies, enabling individuals with motor impairments to
operate devices such as wheelchairs [ 2], thereby improving
independence and quality of life. However, challenges such

To address these challenges, numerous studies have been
conducted to achieve high classiﬁcation accuracies. Early

engineering and separate extraction–classiﬁcation stages,
limiting adaptability and generalization.
Now, Deep learning has enabled end-to-end learning of
spatial–temporal features directly from raw EEG, overcom-
ing the limitations of handcrafted features. Convolutional
neural networks (CNNs), such as EEGNet [ 5], DeepCon-
vNet, and ShallowConvNet [ 6], have been widely applied.
However, their reliance on ﬁxed kernel sizes limits the abil-
ity to capture multi-scale patterns and generalize across
EEG’s inherent variability, often resulting in moderate accu-
racy (68–72%). Recent CNN variants such as FBMSNet,

[14], and temporal convolutional modules that fuse multi-
scale features while modelling both short- and long-term
dependencies [ 15]. SecNet further employs multi-branch
spatio-temporal learning with covariance pooling to enhance
inter-session robustness [ 16]. While effective, such multi-
branch designs often increase model complexity and rarely
explore hold-out subject analysis for better generalization.
Inspired by these studies, we propose the Multi-scale
Convolutional Self-Attention Network (MCSANet), an end-
to-end architecture combining multi-scale temporal convolu-
tions with self-attention to capture long-range dependencies
and enhance feature representation. The key contributions of
this study are as follows:
1. We propose the MCSANet model for multiscale feature
extraction through multiple temporal convolutional layers.
2. We introduce a data augmentation technique, Temporal
Segment Shufﬂing (TSS), for generating additional sample
trials.
3. We integrate MCSANet with the TSS strategy and
evaluate it on the BCIC-IV 2a and 2b datasets under
both subject-dependent and subject-independent settings.
MCSANet outperforms state-of-the-art methods [3, 5, 6, 15–
17],achieving average accuracies of 78.11% and 85.56%
, (subject-dependent) and 63.21% and 78.52% (subject-
independent), respectively.
4. We also conducted an ablation study and visualiza-
tion analysis to assess the individual contributions of TSS,
attention mechanisms, model variants, and learned features,
demonstrating a 5.89% accuracy improvement.
2 Methodology
2.1 Datasets
The BCI Competition (BCIC)-IV -2a dataset [ 18], provided
by Graz University of Technology in 2008, is a widely used
benchmark for MI-EEG classiﬁcation. In this study, the com-
plete dataset, containing data from 9 subjects, was used. EEG
signals were recorded under controlled conditions with min-
imal artifacts, using a sampling frequency of 250 Hz. The
dataset includes signals from 22 electrodes, and each subject
participated in two sessions. Each session comprises 288 tri-
als, resulting in a total of 5,184 trials across all subjects.
The BCIC-IV -2b dataset [19], also provided by Graz Uni-
versity of Technology, was used similarly. The full 2b dataset
includes data from 9 subjects, recorded under controlled con-
ditions with minimal artifacts, and a sampling rate of 250
Hz. Unlike 2a, this dataset includes recordings from only 3
electrodes. Each subject participated in 5 sessions: the train-
ing sessions contain 400 trials„ while the evaluation sessions
include 320 trials for testing.

2.3.2 Temporal segment shufﬂing augmentation
techniques
In MI-EEG classiﬁcation, the limited number of trials
restricts the learning of robust and discriminative features,
reducing accuracy. To address this issue, we propose Tem-
poral Segment Shufﬂing (TSS) an extension to Segmentation
and Recombination (S&R), a data augmentation strategy that
generates new trials by recombining temporal segments from
trials of the same class. TSS enriches intra-class variabil-
ity, strengthens class-speciﬁc representations, and improves
model generalization. We now present a detailed explanation
of the proposed TSS method.
Let X
n denote the nth MI-EEG trial. To generate a new
synthetic trial, we randomly select T trials from the same
class. In this work, we set T = 4, which means that four tri-
als - denoted as X1, X2, X3, and X4 are used in each new trial
generation as shown in Fig. 1. If we select only two trails, the
randomness is signiﬁcantly reduced, resulting in less diverse
data. However, using fewer trails can sometimes lead to more
unique new trails. This creates a trade-off between random-
ness and uniqueness. To achieve a balance, we have chosen
to generate four trails, ensuring sufﬁcient randomness while
maintaining diversity in the new data without any issues.
This value was determined through experimental analysis.
All selected trials come from the same subject and share the
same class label y
n , ensuring class consistency during aug-
mentation.
Segment Selection Strategy: The number of segments S
into which each trial is divided is selected as a multiple of T
(e.g., if T = 4, then S = 4, 8, 12,... ). This ensures that the
segments can be evenly distributed across all selected trials
without leaving any segments unassigned. More generally, if
T = N , then S should be a multiple of N .
Each segment is taken from the same relative time position
in all trials to maintain temporal alignment. This ensures that
the timing and structure of the trail remain consistent within
each segment. The next segment is then taken from another
randomly selected trial, directly after the ﬁrst one, to avoid
losing information or disrupting the signal pattern.

TSS vs. Segmentation and Recombination (S&R) The
proposed TSS follows the same segment-index–aligned
recombination principle as S&R [ 20] but introduces proce-
dural reﬁnements rather than a fundamentally new approach.
In S&R, each trial is divided into (K) non-overlapping seg-
ments, and new trials are formed by concatenating the (k)-th
segment from randomly chosen trials of the same class,
thereby preserving temporal order while increasing variabil-
ity. In contrast, TSS selects 4 trials at a time, with segment
selection tied to trial selection in multiples of 4. When
the number of segments exceeds the number of trials, TSS
applies a cyclic procedure (Eq. [6]) to generate complete
trials. Moreover, unlike S&R, where multiple segments can
originate from the same trial, TSS ensures that no two or
three segments are taken from a single trial, thereby enforcing
greater diversity across the recombined segments. TSS pro-
duces more diverse recombinations and can outperform S&R
in conditions where richer trial diversity improves general-
ization. Experimental results of TSS and S&R are described
in Section IV .
2.4 Network architecture
The proposed DL architecture (Fig. 2) consists of three main
blocks. First, the Convolutional Block extracts features by
applying multi-scale temporal convolutional layers to cap-
ture temporal information, along with depthwise convolution
to learn spatial patterns. Next, the Attention Block takes
these extracted features and selectively emphasizes the most
relevant spatial-temporal components that contribute to the
motor imagery (MI) task. Finally, the Classiﬁcation Block
processes the reﬁned features and assigns them to the correct
class using a softmax activation function.
2.4.1 Convolutional block
In the proposed MCSANet architecture, we incorporate
multiple temporal convolutional layers to capture a richer
hierarchy of temporal features from the input MI-EEG sig-
nals. To achieve this, we utilize a stack of three 2D temporal
convolutional layers, each designed with a different ﬁlter size
corresponding to varying temporal resolutions. Speciﬁcally,
ﬁlter lengths ( k) are set to half, approximately one-fourth,
and one-eighth of the sampling rate. This approach enables
the model to capture temporal patterns across different time-
point scales. Each layer uses the same number of ﬁlters,
denoted as F

2.4.2 Attention block
MI-EEG signals exhibit synchronization over time, which
can be analyzed using coherence measures to reveal connec-
tions in brain activity patterns. Since these patterns involve
relationships both across time and across channels, effec-
tive decoding requires capturing both temporal and spatial
features. While CNNs are widely used for this task, their
limited receptive ﬁeld restricts their ability to capture long-
range dependencies. Self-attention addresses this by learning
long-term relationships that CNNs may overlook. Simply,
self-attention works by focusing on the most relevant parts
of the input data, similar to how human attention prioritizes
important elements while ignoring less important ones. The
mechanism uses three components: queries (features to focus
on), keys (cues to measure relevance), and values (input fea-
tures). Queries and keys interact to calculate attention scores,
which determine the importance of each value. Using these
scores, the self-attention block improves the model’s ability
to focus on meaningful features, improving performance in
decoding complex MI-EEG patterns.

3 Results and discussion
We evaluated the performance of the proposed MCSANet
model using two key metrics: classiﬁcation accuracy and
Cohen’s kappa score. MCSANet is compared against several
state-of-the-art models, including EEGNet [5], ShallowCon-
vNet and DeepConvNet [ 6], TCANet [ 15], TCNet Fusion
[17], and A TCNet [3].
3.1 Training setup
MCSANet was evaluated using a 5-fold cross-validation
scheme under both subject-dependent and subject-
independent settings. In the subject-dependent setup incor-
porating a hold-out session analysis, data from each sub-
ject’s training session were used for training, while data
from their corresponding evaluation session were used for
testing, following the original dataset protocol. For the
subject-independent setup, we adopted a leave-one-subject-
out (LOSO) approach, from a total of n subjects, data
from n − 1 subjects were used for training, and the one
subject was used for testing. This process was repeated
n times, each time leaving out a different subject for
evaluation.
The model was trained using the Glorot uniform initial-
izer for weight initialization and the Adam optimizer with
a learning rate of 0.001. Training was conducted with a
batch size of 16 using categorical cross-entropy loss over
300 epochs. These hyperparameters were ﬁne-tuned through
multiple experiments to enhance generalization. Table S2
summarizes the hyperparameter search procedure. To ensure
fair comparison, the same training setup was applied to all
state-of-the-art models evaluated.

3.3 Impact of data augmentation
To evaluate the effectiveness of TSS-based data augmenta-
tion (DA) on the performance of MCSANet, we conducted
a comparative analysis of classiﬁcation results with and
without DA, as shown in Table 4, alongside various state-of-
the-art models. The results clearly indicate that the absence
of data augmentation causes a noticeable decline in per-
formance across all evaluated models. Despite this decline,
MCSANet continues to outperform most models, except for
A TCNet. However, when DA is applied, MCSANet surpasses
all state-of-the-art models, including A TCNet. Speciﬁcally,
the inclusion of DA yields an approximate 5% improve-
ment in accuracy, highlighting its signiﬁcant contribution to
enhancing classiﬁcation performance. To evaluate the effec-
tiveness of TSS, we also compared it with another data
augmentation approach, S&R. The results indicate that TSS
produces statistically similar results to S&R.
3.4 Impact of trail segmentation
In the TSS, as discussed in Section III-B, we select 4 random
trials (denoted as X1, X2, X3, and X4) to ensure a good bal-
ance between randomness and uniqueness in the generated
data. The number of new trials Xnew can be large, depending
on how many combinations are made. Also, each trial can
be divided into several segments, necessitating a multiple of
4 (selected trials). So, both segmentation and the number of
new trials are important in the DA process.
To analyze the impact of new trail generation on test
accuracy, we compare the proposed model MCSANet, in
terms of both segment selection and new trail generation.
Table 5 presents a comparison of the output based on these
two factors, highlighting their inﬂuence on model perfor-
mance. As a result, we increased the number of newly
generated trials, but this did not lead to improved accu-
racy. This might happen because adding too much synthetic
data gives fewer useful features and can even add noise
or repeated information. Similarly, while larger segmen-
tation increases data variability, it also introduces more
randomness and may fail to capture the interactions between
features at different scales, ultimately leading to a decrease in
accuracy.
Considering both datasets, the number of newly generated
trials impacted model performance differently. For the BCI-
IV -2a dataset, generating 200 new trials yielded the highest
accuracy, whereas for the BCI-IV -2b dataset, the best results
were achieved with 100 new trials. However, to maintain
consistency and support the concept of generalizability, we
adopted a uniform strategy: selecting 4 segments and gen-
erating 200 new trials across all state-of-the-art techniques
evaluated in this study.

4 Conclusion
This work introduces MCSANet, a novel DL model that
integrates a multi-scale temporal convolutional layer with
an attention mechanism and novel data augmentation tech-
niques TSS to enhance MI-EEG classiﬁcation. A compar-
ative evaluation against state-of-the-art DL models, viz.,
EEGNet, ShallowConvNet, DeepConvNet, TCANet, TCNet
Fusion, A TCNet, and SecNet demonstrate that MCSANet
consistently outperforms these models across both datasets.
MCSANet achieves accuracies of 78.11% and 85.56%, with
kappa scores of 0.72 for both, on the BCI Competition IV -
2a and IV -2b datasets, respectively. The proposed TSS-based
data augmentation enhances the performance of all evaluated
models, conﬁrming its effectiveness in improving MI-EEG
signal classiﬁcation accuracy. Notably, MCSANet exhibits a
5.89% increase in accuracy with TSS. In addition, an abla-
tion analysis of different network components shows that the
attention mechanism improves classiﬁcation performance.
The above results underscore the robustness and reliability of
MCSANet, making it more suitable for practical BCI appli-
cations.
In future work, we plan to explore alternative strategies
focused on generating synthetic data, aiming to improve the
model’s ability to learn underlying patterns and enhance its
generalizability.
